# Glaucoma Intervention Analysis: Insights Summary
**Intervention:** Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)
**Analysis Date:** 2026-04-11
**Dataset:** 16 eyes, 15 patients, 8 source studies (all verified)

---

## 1. Key Finding

SLT produced a statistically significant, large-magnitude reduction in intraocular pressure (mean −7.1 mmHg; −27.7%), with 75% of eyes achieving the clinically accepted threshold of ≥20% IOP reduction.

---

## 2. Efficacy Summary

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Mean IOP pre-treatment | 26.2 ± 6.9 mmHg |
| Mean IOP post-treatment | 19.1 ± 7.3 mmHg |
| Mean absolute IOP change | −7.1 mmHg (95% CI: −9.2 to −5.0 mmHg) |
| Mean percent IOP change | −27.7% (SD ±12.7%) |
| Median percent IOP change | −32.7% |

**Statistical significance:** Paired t-test: t = −7.31, p = 2.55 × 10⁻⁶ (p < 0.001), indicating a highly significant reduction in IOP following SLT.

**Effect size:** Cohen's d (paired) = −1.83, interpreted as a **large** effect. This indicates that the observed IOP reduction is not only statistically significant but also substantially greater in magnitude than would be expected by chance or measurement noise alone.

The IOP change distribution was consistent with normality (Shapiro-Wilk W = 0.93, p = 0.24), supporting the validity of the parametric test applied.

---

## 3. Clinical Relevance

A mean reduction of 7.1 mmHg (27.7%) from a pre-treatment baseline of 26.2 mmHg is clinically meaningful. In glaucoma management, a ≥20% IOP reduction from baseline is the widely used threshold for defining clinical success, as this level of reduction has been associated with slowing optic nerve damage and visual field progression in landmark trials (e.g., AGIS, CIGTS).

The mean percent reduction of 27.7% exceeds this threshold at the group level, and the 95% confidence interval for mean change (−9.2 to −5.0 mmHg) excludes zero by a wide margin, reinforcing the robustness of the effect. The large Cohen's d (1.83) suggests the signal is strong relative to inter-patient variability, which is notable given the inherent heterogeneity of glaucoma populations.

Caution is warranted, however, in generalizing from this small, aggregated case series to the broader population. See Limitations below.

---

## 4. Responder Analysis

- **12 of 16 eyes (75.0%)** achieved ≥20% IOP reduction, meeting the standard clinical success criterion.
- The remaining 4 eyes (25.0%) had reductions below this threshold; the minimum observed change was −2.6%, indicating that all eyes showed at least some degree of IOP lowering.
- The range of percent change (−2.6% to −41.2%) reflects meaningful inter-individual variability in SLT response, consistent with the published literature.

A 75% responder rate is a clinically relevant outcome metric and should be highlighted in any formal report to contextualize the group-level statistics.

---

## 5. Medication Impact

SLT was associated with a statistically significant reduction in the number of concurrent glaucoma medications:

| Metric | Pre-SLT | Post-SLT |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Mean medications | 2.44 ± 0.73 | 1.75 ± 1.06 |
| Median medications | 2.0 | 1.0 |
| Range | 2–4 | 1–4 |
| Mean change | −0.69 ± 0.48 medications |

**Wilcoxon signed-rank test:** W = 0.0, p = 0.0009 (p < 0.001). The non-parametric test was used for the medication data and confirms a highly significant reduction in medication burden.

This finding suggests that SLT may reduce patient medication load, with potential benefits for adherence, tolerability, and cost of care. A median reduction of 1.0 medication per eye is a clinically meaningful adjunctive benefit beyond IOP lowering alone.

---

## 6. Limitations

- **Sample size:** The analysis is based on 16 eyes from 15 patients across 8 source studies. This is a small aggregate series; estimates carry wide uncertainty and results should be interpreted with appropriate caution.
- **Study design:** Data were compiled from case reports and clinical notes rather than a prospective randomized trial. Selection bias, varying follow-up protocols, and heterogeneous documentation practices may influence findings.
- **Follow-up heterogeneity:** Follow-up durations were not reported as uniform; variability in time-to-post-IOP measurement may affect the comparability of post-intervention values.
- **Diagnosis mix:** The cohort includes primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG, n=10), pseudoexfoliation glaucoma (n=2), normal-tension glaucoma (NTG, n=2), and pigmentary glaucoma (n=2). These subtypes may respond differently to SLT, and the mixed sample limits subgroup-level conclusions.
- **Verification status:** All 16 eyes were flagged as verified by the pipeline's verification agent. However, verification was performed algorithmically against extracted data, not through independent clinical re-abstraction.
- **No control group:** Absence of a concurrent untreated or sham control group precludes causal attribution of IOP change to SLT alone.

---

## 7. Comparison to Literature

The results from this series are broadly consistent with published SLT outcomes:

- **IOP reduction:** Meta-analyses of SLT (e.g., Rolim de Moura et al., 2007; Garg & Gazzard, 2018) typically report mean IOP reductions of 5–8 mmHg (20–30%) at 6–12 months, comparable to the −7.1 mmHg (27.7%) observed here.
- **Responder rate:** Published responder rates (≥20% IOP reduction) range from 60–85% at 6–12 months. The 75% rate in this cohort falls within this expected range.
- **Medication reduction:** SLT's ability to reduce medication burden is well-documented; the LiGHT trial (Gazzard et al., 2019) demonstrated that SLT-first management kept approximately 74% of eyes at target IOP without medication at 3 years in treatment-naive patients.
- **Normal-tension glaucoma:** The 2 NTG eyes in this cohort represent a subtype where SLT evidence is less robust; published data suggest more modest responses, though this subgroup is too small to assess separately here.

Overall, the observed outcomes are within the range reported in the peer-reviewed literature, lending face validity to the extraction and analysis pipeline, while acknowledging the limitations of aggregated case-level data.

---

## 8. Recommendations

1. **Clinical interpretation:** The current evidence from this series supports SLT as an effective IOP-lowering intervention in this mixed-diagnosis cohort, with a response profile consistent with the published literature. These findings may be used to support treatment planning discussions.

2. **Expand the dataset:** To improve precision and enable subgroup analysis (by diagnosis, baseline IOP, or medication class), additional case reports or prospective records should be incorporated. A minimum of 30–50 eyes per subgroup would be needed for reliable subgroup-level inference.

3. **Stratified analysis:** Future analyses should stratify by glaucoma subtype (POAG vs. NTG vs. pseudoexfoliation vs. pigmentary), given potential differences in SLT mechanism and response across these entities.

4. **Non-responder characterization:** The 25% of eyes that did not achieve ≥20% reduction warrant further investigation. Baseline IOP, diagnosis, and prior medication history should be examined as potential predictors of non-response.

5. **Longitudinal follow-up:** SLT efficacy is known to diminish over time (median durability ~2–5 years). Any formal report should clearly document follow-up duration and note that longer-term outcome data are needed.

6. **Repeat SLT consideration:** For patients who responded initially, SLT retreatment is a recognized option. Medication reduction outcomes here suggest that some patients may benefit from a treat-to-target strategy anchored by SLT with adjunctive medications titrated down.
