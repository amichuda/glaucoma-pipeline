# Case Report Outline: Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)

**Prepared by:** Academic Medical Writer (Ophthalmology)
**Date:** 2026-04-11
**Intervention:** Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)
**Dataset:** 16 eyes, 15 patients, 8 source documents (5 case reports, 3 clinical notes); all records verified

## 1. Title

**Proposed Title:**
*Intraocular Pressure Reduction and Medication Burden Following Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty: A Multi-Source Aggregated Case Series*

**Alternative title options:**

## 2. Abstract (Structured)

### Purpose
- To evaluate the intraocular pressure (IOP)-lowering efficacy and medication-reduction benefit of Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT) across a mixed-diagnosis glaucoma cohort derived from aggregated case reports and clinical notes.
- To quantify the proportion of eyes achieving the established clinical success threshold (≥20% IOP reduction) and to assess medication load changes following SLT.

### Methods
- Retrospective aggregated case series: 16 eyes of 15 patients (mean age 61.1 years; 9 male, 6 female) drawn from 8 source documents.
- Glaucoma subtypes: primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG, n=10 eyes), pseudoexfoliation glaucoma (n=2), normal-tension glaucoma (NTG, n=2), pigmentary glaucoma (n=2).
- Follow-up ranged from 1 to 56.5 weeks. Paired t-test applied to IOP data (normality confirmed by Shapiro-Wilk); Wilcoxon signed-rank test for medication count data.

### Results (key numbers)
- Mean IOP decreased from 26.2 ± 6.9 mmHg pre-SLT to 19.1 ± 7.3 mmHg post-SLT; mean absolute change −7.1 mmHg (95% CI: −9.2 to −5.0 mmHg; p < 0.001; Cohen's d = −1.83, large effect).
- 12 of 16 eyes (75.0%) achieved ≥20% IOP reduction.
- Mean glaucoma medications decreased from 2.44 ± 0.73 to 1.75 ± 1.06 per eye (mean change −0.69; Wilcoxon p < 0.001).

### Conclusions
- SLT produced a statistically significant, large-magnitude reduction in IOP alongside a clinically meaningful decrease in medication burden; the 75% responder rate is consistent with published literature.
- Findings support SLT as an effective first- or adjunctive-line intervention in a mixed-diagnosis glaucoma population, while acknowledging the inherent limitations of aggregated case-level data and the absence of a control group.

## 3. Introduction

### Background on Glaucoma and IOP
- Glaucoma is the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide; elevated IOP remains the primary modifiable risk factor and the target of all approved pharmacological and procedural therapies.
- Landmark trials (AGIS, CIGTS, OHTS) established that sustained IOP reductions of ≥20–30% from baseline are associated with slowing optic nerve damage and visual field progression.
- The heterogeneity of glaucoma subtypes — including POAG, pseudoexfoliation, normal-tension, and pigmentary forms — creates variability in treatment response that underscores the need for real-world outcome data.

### Rationale for SLT
- SLT (Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty) selectively targets pigmented trabecular meshwork cells using a frequency-doubled, Q-switched Nd:YAG laser (532 nm), stimulating macrophage-mediated remodeling of the trabecular meshwork without the collateral thermal damage associated with argon laser trabeculoplasty (ALT).
- The LiGHT trial (Gazzard et al., 2019, *Lancet*) demonstrated that SLT-first management kept ~74% of treatment-naive eyes at target IOP without medications at 3 years, establishing SLT as a first-line option.
- SLT offers the additional advantage of repeatability and freedom from daily drop-related adherence and tolerability burdens.

### Study Objective
- To describe IOP outcomes and medication changes following SLT in a mixed-diagnosis cohort assembled from aggregated case-level data, and to benchmark these against published evidence.
- [**Data note:** The study objective is adequately supported by the available dataset; however, subgroup-level analysis by glaucoma diagnosis is limited by small cell sizes (n=2 per non-POAG subtype).]

## 4. Case Description / Methods

### Patient Demographics
- 15 patients (16 eyes: 8 OD, 8 OS); mean age 61.1 years (range 47–82 years).
- Sex: 9 male (60%), 6 female (40%).
- Glaucoma diagnosis distribution: POAG n=10 eyes (62.5%), pseudoexfoliation n=2 (12.5%), normal-tension glaucoma n=2 (12.5%), pigmentary glaucoma n=2 (12.5%).

### Baseline Findings
- Pre-SLT IOP: mean 26.2 ± 6.9 mmHg (median 25.0 mmHg; range 16–38 mmHg; IQR 22.0–29.5 mmHg).
- Pre-SLT glaucoma medications: mean 2.44 ± 0.73 (median 2.0; range 2–4).
- Baseline IOP range reflects a clinically heterogeneous cohort, including two NTG eyes (baseline IOP 16 mmHg) and one eye with markedly elevated IOP (38 mmHg, pigmentary glaucoma).

### Intervention Details
- All patients underwent SLT as documented in source case reports and clinical notes.
- [**Data note:** Specific laser parameters (energy per pulse, number of spots, degrees of angle treated) were not uniformly reported across source documents and could not be extracted for inclusion. This is an acknowledged limitation of the retrospective case-series design.]

### Follow-up Protocol
- Follow-up assessments ranged from 1 week to 56.5 weeks (approximately 13 months) post-SLT.
- Multiple time-point measurements were available for some patients (e.g., case_report_01: 4 weeks and 13 weeks; case_report_03: 2 months and 13 months).
- [**Data note:** Follow-up heterogeneity is a limitation; post-SLT IOP values were not collected at uniform intervals across all source documents, which limits time-series analysis and conclusions about durability.]

### Statistical Methods
- Normality of IOP change distribution assessed by Shapiro-Wilk test (W = 0.93, p = 0.24; normal distribution confirmed).
- Primary efficacy test: Wilcoxon paired t-test on pre- vs. post-SLT IOP.
- Effect size: Cohen's d (paired samples).
- 95% CI for mean IOP change computed using t-distribution.
- Medication burden change assessed by Wilcoxon signed-rank test (non-parametric, due to ordinal/count nature of medication data).
- Clinical success defined as ≥20% IOP reduction from baseline (standard criterion per AGIS/CIGTS benchmarks).

## 5. Results

### Primary Outcome: IOP Reduction
- Mean IOP reduced from 26.2 ± 6.9 mmHg to 19.1 ± 7.3 mmHg; mean absolute reduction −7.1 mmHg (95% CI: −9.2 to −5.0 mmHg).
- Mean percent IOP reduction: −27.7% (SD ±12.7%); median −32.7% (IQR: −35.6% to −23.4%).
- Paired t-test: t(15) = −7.31, p = 2.55 × 10⁻⁶ (p < 0.001); Cohen's d = −1.83 (large effect size).

### Responder Analysis
- 12 of 16 eyes (75.0%) achieved the clinical success criterion of ≥20% IOP reduction.
- The 4 non-responder eyes (25.0%) still demonstrated IOP lowering; the minimum observed reduction was −2.6%, indicating no eye experienced IOP elevation post-SLT in this cohort.
- IOP percent change ranged from −2.6% to −41.2%, demonstrating meaningful inter-individual variability consistent with published SLT literature.

### Medication Changes
- Mean glaucoma medications per eye decreased from 2.44 ± 0.73 (pre-SLT) to 1.75 ± 1.06 (post-SLT); mean change −0.69 ± 0.48 medications.
- Median medications decreased from 2.0 to 1.0 per eye (range pre: 2–4; range post: 1–4).
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test: W = 0.0, p = 0.0009 (p < 0.001), confirming statistically significant reduction in medication burden.

### Adverse Events
- [**Data note:** Adverse event data (e.g., IOP spike, anterior chamber reaction, corneal changes) were not systematically reported across source documents and cannot be summarized quantitatively. ## 6. Discussion

### Comparison to Published Literature
- The mean IOP reduction of −7.1 mmHg (−27.7%) is consistent with published SLT meta-analyses reporting 5–8 mmHg (20–30%) reductions at 6–12 months (Rolim de Moura et al., 2007; Garg & Gazzard, 2018).
- The 75% responder rate (≥20% IOP reduction) falls within the reported published range of 60–85% at 6–12 months in prior SLT trials and observational series.
- The LiGHT trial (Gazzard et al., 2019) found SLT-first management maintained target IOP without medications in ~74% of eyes at 3 years, providing an important benchmark for the medication-reduction finding observed here (mean −0.69 medications/eye; median −1.0).

### Mechanism of Action
- SLT's IOP-lowering effect is attributed to laser-induced macrophage activation and trabecular meshwork remodeling, enhancing aqueous outflow through the conventional pathway.
- Unlike ALT, SLT spares non-pigmented cells, allowing for retreatment without cumulative thermal damage — a property relevant when interpreting the long-term follow-up data (up to 56.5 weeks) in this cohort.
- The heterogeneous response (−2.6% to −41.2%) is thought to reflect variation in trabecular pigmentation, outflow facility, and potential genetic factors influencing laser-tissue interaction.

### Strengths
- All 16 eyes were pipeline-verified, providing internally consistent data with standardized field extraction.
- Large Cohen's d (1.83) indicates that the IOP-lowering signal is robust relative to inter-patient variability, lending confidence to the directional conclusion despite the small sample size.
- The mixed-diagnosis cohort (POAG, pseudoexfoliation, NTG, pigmentary) reflects a real-world glaucoma population, enhancing generalizability to clinical practice settings.

### Limitations
- Small sample size (16 eyes, 15 patients) from 8 heterogeneous source documents limits statistical precision and precludes subgroup analysis by diagnosis.
- Retrospective aggregated case-series design: selection bias, variable documentation quality, and non-uniform follow-up intervals may influence results.
- Absence of a control group precludes causal attribution; IOP regression to the mean cannot be excluded, particularly in eyes with high baseline IOP.
- Laser parameters were not uniformly reported and could not be analyzed as covariates.
- The two NTG eyes (baseline IOP 16 mmHg) represent a subtype where SLT evidence is less established; their inclusion in a pooled analysis may limit applicability of findings to hypertensive glaucoma populations.
- [**Data note:** The pigmentary glaucoma patient (P11/P12; both time points) showed essentially no IOP response (38 → 37 mmHg at 1 week; 38 → 36 mmHg at 2 months), which is a notable clinical observation and should be discussed explicitly as an outlier case.]

### Clinical Implications
- A mean −27.7% IOP reduction and 75% responder rate support SLT as a viable therapeutic option in mixed-diagnosis glaucoma, consistent with guidelines recommending SLT as first-line or adjunctive treatment.
- The statistically significant median reduction of 1.0 medication per eye suggests potential for reducing polypharmacy burden, with downstream benefits for patient adherence, quality of life, and healthcare costs.
- The outlier pigmentary glaucoma non-responder underscores the importance of close post-SLT follow-up and individualized reassessment, particularly in patients where trabecular meshwork characteristics may limit laser efficacy.

---

## 7. Conclusion

- SLT produced a statistically significant, large-magnitude reduction in IOP (mean −7.1 mmHg; −27.7%; p < 0.001; Cohen's d = −1.83) in a mixed-diagnosis cohort of 16 eyes across 8 source case reports.
- Seventy-five percent of eyes achieved the standard clinical success threshold of ≥20% IOP reduction, a responder rate consistent with the published SLT literature.
- A concomitant, statistically significant reduction in glaucoma medication burden (median −1.0 medication/eye; p < 0.001) reinforces the clinical utility of SLT beyond IOP control alone.
- These findings support recommending SLT as an effective IOP-lowering and medication-sparing intervention, while prospective controlled studies with larger samples, uniform follow-up intervals, and standardized laser protocols are needed to fully characterize long-term efficacy across glaucoma subtypes.
